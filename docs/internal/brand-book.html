02Voice

Four qualities. Same voice, every format.

Whether it’s a 2-line LinkedIn post, a 12-slide carousel or a 700-word newsletter, the voice keeps the same four properties. Tone shifts; voice doesn’t.

01
Empathetic but expert.
Acknowledge the emotional weight of job searching. Never sentimental. Always offers direction — never preachy.
02
Direct and concise.
Short sentences. No filler. Every line earns its place. If it can be said in 5 words, it doesn’t get said in 15.
03
Company voice, never first person.
Unimad speaks as a platform, a team, a system — never as “I.” First person only for founders, students, or direct quotes.
04
Warm but not casual.
Between supportive mentor and sharp strategist. Contractions are fine. Slang is not.
03Personality

Five characters. One is never used.

Two primary, one seasoning, one background, one off-limits. Pick a personality before drafting, not after — it changes the opening line.

A
Primary
The sharp friend.
Dry wit. Says what everyone is thinking but nobody posts. Roasts the situation, never the person. Humour makes the point land harder.
“Applying to 100 jobs with the same resume is not a strategy. That’s a lottery ticket with worse odds.”
“Recruiters remember clarity. ATS systems reward relevance. The inbox responds to specificity.”
Use for
Educational carousels · Contrarian takes · Punchy single-frame posts
E
Primary
The been-there elder sibling.
Warm. Knowing. Slightly teasing. The humour is recognitional, not clever. Speaks like someone who lived through it and came out the other side.
“Every job seeker has a ‘985’ story. Some are still in it.”
“The best career advice is boring. Update the resume. Personalise every email. Follow up. That’s it. That’s the secret.”
Use for
Mad Stories · Building in public · Newsletters
D
Seasoning · ~1 in 5
The absurdist realist.
Used sparingly. Points out the ridiculous parts of job searching with deadpan honesty. The humour comes from the truth.
“Job search tip #47: Refresh the inbox every 3 minutes. It won’t help, but it feels productive.”
“Step 1: Optimise LinkedIn. Step 2: Optimise the resume. Step 3: Optimise cold emails. Step 4: Realise three weeks have passed and zero applications have gone out.”
Use for
Polls · This-or-that · Meme days · Playful intros
B
Background
The knowing observer.
Calm, slightly amused, one step ahead. Surfaces for the smartest closing lines and newsletter tone. Authority over warmth.
“Random applications are dying. Focused job search is winning.”
Use for
Data insights · Product demos · Authoritative closers
C
Never
The tough-love coach.
Unimad does not lecture, shame, or talk down to its audience. Confrontational energy is off-brand. Sharpness comes from wit — not aggression. If a line could make a struggling job seeker feel worse about themselves, it gets cut.
Cut on sight
“You need to hustle harder.” · “Stop making excuses.” · “Quit complaining and apply.”

05Brevity

Density over length.

Unimad does not bore. Every post should feel like it respects the reader’s time. If something can be said in 5 words, it does not get said in 15.

Text posts
150–250 words max.
Shorter than most LinkedIn advice. Earn attention through density, not length. No padding.
Carousels
1 idea per slide.
If a slide needs a paragraph, it’s two slides. White space is pacing, not waste.
Formatting
One line per thought.
LinkedIn is read on phones. Short lines, frequent breaks. Dense paragraphs are invisible.
Cut the warm-up
No “In today’s world…”
No “As we all know…” or “It’s no secret that…” Start with the point, the tension or the number.
The 3-second test
First 2 lines must earn the click.
If they don’t earn a “See more” tap, the post fails. Every hook gets pressure-tested before publish.
Kill words
really · very · just · actually
Plus “basically,” “in order to,” every adverb. If the sentence works without the word, the word goes.
06Voice rules

Seven things we don’t do.

Non-negotiable on every post, regardless of channel. These are the moves that break the voice from one post to the next.

01
No first person.
Never “I,” “me,” “my,” or “our” outside a direct quote. Use “Unimad,” “the team,” “the platform,” or “you.”
02
Use hashtags sparingly.
Brand hashtags are optional, not default. Use only when contextually relevant.
03
No external links in the body.
Links cut reach by 60%. Use “DM for the link” or “comment below” instead.
04
End with a strong takeaway or a question.
Either a specific, answerable question or a strong closing takeaway. Not every post needs a question — but every post needs a clear ending.
05
Value first, always.
Every post should offer value. Product mentions should feel earned, not forced.
06
Product name mid-post only.
Unimad, Unibot or Unicoach appears once, naturally, inside a narrative — never the last line. Never a slogan.
07
No engagement bait.
“Agree?”, “Comment YES,” “Like if you relate” trigger spam detection and suppress reach. If a question needs that scaffolding, it’s the wrong question.
07Post structure

Hook. Body. Question.

Every post follows this three-part structure. No exceptions, no preambles, no slogan-style sign-offs.

Part 1
01
The hook.
First 2 lines. LinkedIn truncates at ~210 characters. Creates curiosity, tension or recognition. Never a product name. Never generic.
“492 rejections. A warehouse job. Three months left.”
Part 2
02
The body.
150–250 words for text. 5–10 slides for carousels. One clear takeaway the reader can act on today. This is where Unimad earns the right to exist in someone’s feed.
~ ONE IDEA · ONE TAKEAWAY · ONE ACTION ~
Part 3
03
The question.
Last line. Specific. Answerable. The more specific the question, the more comments it generates.
“How many of your applications this week were actually tailored?”
08Do vs. don’t

The same idea, two ways.

Most off-brand lines aren’t wrong — they’re generic. Compare side-by-side until the rewrites become muscle memory.

Don’t
Cold emails feel awkward. #IamUnibot
Do
Cold emails feel awkward. Here’s the opening line that got one student 12 replies. [Framework] … What’s the worst cold email you’ve sent?
Don’t
Just Unimad it.
Do
The students who land jobs don’t apply more. They apply differently. What would you change about your strategy?
Don’t
I built Unimad because I lived through this.
Do
Unimad exists because its founders lived through this — the ghosting, the visa pressure, the 500th rejection.
Don’t
In today’s competitive job market, it is essential to have a strong personal brand that…
Do
Personal branding isn’t a luxury. It’s the job search.
Don’t
Our tool is designed to help job seekers optimise their applications efficiently.
Do
The platform handles formatting, keywords, and ATS checks. The job seeker handles strategy.
09SEO & discoverability

LinkedIn is now a search engine.

Posts get found through LinkedIn’s internal search and Google. The first two lines do the SEO work; the final question drives the engagement that re-ranks the post.

LinkedIn internal search
  • Place target keywords naturally in the first 2 lines of every post.
  • Use terms the target audience actually searches.
  • High engagement increases search ranking — the closing question is also an SEO play.
Google indexing
  • Newsletters get unique URLs that Google indexes — keyword-rich title & first paragraph.
  • LinkedIn articles are also indexed; regular posts only inside LinkedIn.
  • Google pulls the first 150–160 characters as the snippet. Make them count.

11Visual principles

Six rules that keep every visual on system.

The visual rules trade on credibility. We earn it by being concrete, sequenced and visibly restrained — not by stacking gradients or quoting motivation.

01 — Specificity

Always lead with a real number, a real role, a real outcome.

“500 applications. 0 offers.” beats “lots of struggle”. If you can’t cite it, it doesn’t make the post.

02 — Restraint

One idea per frame. One CTA per carousel.

Carousels are sequential. Each frame advances the argument exactly one step — never two.

03 — Annotation, not decoration

Use the red marker to point at the point.

Red underlines, circles and arrows pull the eye to the single most important phrase. Never to “add a vibe”.

04 — Three surfaces

White by default. Dark for stakes. Blue to close.

Body / argument / list frames go on white. Cover and proof frames go dark. The carousel closes on full blue. Gradients and blurred glows are allowed — but only in brand colours, on these three.

05 — One typeface

Onest, top to bottom. Weight does the work.

300/400 for body, 500 for emphasis. We don’t reach for a second display face — the U mark already carries the brand.

06 — Signed by the U

Every frame closes with the U mark and the URL.

U mark top-right, 80px clearance. Wordmark lockup centred when used. www.unimad.ai bottom-center. No exceptions across the carousel.

13Surfaces

Three surfaces. Pure white, dark, blue.

The system codifies only three backgrounds. Gradients and blurred glows are permitted on top of these — but only using brand colours. Surface treatments like ripples, waves or grid overlays are seasonal trends and don’t belong in the brand book.

01 · WHITE
The default body surface. Most of the carousel lives here.
#FFFFFF
White
Use for: body / argument / list frames. Clarity, density, marker annotations.
02 · DARK
Near-black. Carries stakes and proof frames.
#0A0E1A
Dark
Use for: cover frames, stakes-setting, recaps. White type, Blue 600 highlights.
03 · BLUE
Unimad Blue 600. The sign-off surface.
#346DE0
Blue
Use for: blue closer with the comment-keyword CTA. One per carousel, never more.
Permitted decoration
Gradients and blurred glows are allowed — but only using brand colours.
Dark → blue gradient
A soft linear gradient from near-black to brand blue. Replaces the older radial blur — cleaner, more predictable, prints better.
BLUE + BLUE GRADIENT
Blue 500→700 radial on the blue surface. Adds depth on closer frames without leaving the brand ramp.
WHITE + BLUE WASH
Blue 050 wash drifting in from a corner. Subtle — never strong enough to read as a second surface.
Do
Layer gradients and blurred glows in brand blue on top of the three base surfaces. Keep it soft, single-anchor, and aligned to the focal element.
Don’t
Don’t codify trend treatments (ripples, waves, grid overlays, halftone, noise) into the system. Use them per-campaign if you must, but they don’t live in the brand book.
Rule
Within a single carousel: dark cover → 4–6 white body frames → blue closer. Decoration on a surface should never read as a second surface.

20Tone & voice cheat-sheet

Founder-direct. Coach-clear.

If a sentence couldn’t be said over a coffee with a student, it doesn’t belong on the post. Avoid LinkedIn-ese, hustle-vocabulary, and motivational platitudes.

Do say

  • “Pooja applied to over 1,000 jobs before realising this.”
  • “It’s not a market problem. It’s a positioning problem.”
  • “If your CV doesn’t match your LinkedIn, that’s your bottleneck.”
  • “Comment ‘UK’ and we’ll send the framework.”

Don’t say

  • “Unlock your potential and crush your next opportunity!”
  • “In today’s competitive landscape…”
  • “Game-changing tips that will transform your career.”
  • Anything starting with “Here are 7 reasons why…”
Do

Lead with a real person, a real number, and a real outcome. Carousels follow a 6-beat narrative: cover → proof → diagnosis → fix → recap → call.

Use the red marker on exactly one phrase per frame, and never more than three times across a carousel.

Don’t

Don’t introduce secondary brand colours, gradient text, or motivational metaphors. The blue ramp and ink ramp are sufficient.

Don’t stack quotation marks, bullets and stat blocks on the same frame — pick one per frame.

21A note on change

Design has eras. This book will need updating.

The brand fundamentals — who Unimad is, the voice, the personality, the U mark, the blue, the Onest type — should change rarely, if ever. The visual surface is different. Treatments, layouts, decoration trends and even templates have a shelf life. Plan to revisit this document.

Treat as permanent
  • The U mark and the “unimad” wordmark lockup.
  • Unimad Blue 600 as the single brand colour.
  • Onest as the only typeface.
  • The four voice qualities, five personalities, vocabulary.
  • The lowercase tracked URL footer treatment.
Expect to update
  • Background treatments, gradients, glows.
  • Specific carousel templates and frame archetypes.
  • Decorative components — cards, pills, illustrations.
  • Marker annotation conventions if visual trends shift.
  • SEO keyword lists — refresh every 6 months.
